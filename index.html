Industry 101: How Childcare & Camps Work

If you're new to this industry, here's a quick primer on how these businesses operate and why software matters.

The Childcare Business Model

How Childcare Centers Make Money
  • Revenue: Monthly tuition ($800-2,500/child/month depending on location and age)
  • Capacity: Most centers serve 50-200 children
  • Staffing: State-mandated ratios (e.g., 1 teacher per 4 infants, 1 per 10 preschoolers)
  • Margins: Typically 10-20% net margin ‚Äî labor is the biggest cost
  • Retention: Families stay 2-4 years on average (until kindergarten)
üìã Daily Operations

Check kids in/out, track meals and naps, log diaper changes, record learning activities, take photos, communicate with parents throughout the day.

üíµ Billing Challenges

Collect tuition (often late), handle subsidies, track late pickup fees, manage sibling discounts, reconcile payments. Cash flow is constant struggle.

üìù Compliance

State licensing requires staff-to-child ratios, health records, immunization tracking, incident reports, and regular inspections.

üë®‚Äçüë©‚Äçüëß Parent Expectations

Modern parents expect real-time updates, photos, and an app. They compare centers partly based on how "professional" the technology looks.

The Camp Business Model

How Camps Make Money
  • Revenue: Per-session fees ($200-600/week for day camps, $1,000-3,000/week for overnight)
  • Seasonality: Most revenue in summer (June-August), some break camps
  • Registration: Opens January-February, peak sign-ups by April
  • Capacity: Day camps: 50-500 campers/week. Overnight: 100-1,000 per session.
  • Margins: 15-30% for well-run camps ‚Äî higher than childcare
üóìÔ∏è Session-Based

Unlike childcare's ongoing enrollment, camps sell discrete sessions (Week 1, Week 2, etc.) with different themes, activities, or age groups.

üé® Activity Selection

Campers often choose activities (swimming, archery, arts) or "tracks" (sports track, STEM track). Registration is more complex than childcare.

üí≥ Payment Plans

Camps collect deposits in winter, balance due before camp starts. Payment plans, early bird discounts, and sibling discounts are common.

üì∏ Parent Communication

Less frequent than childcare ‚Äî end-of-day photos rather than real-time updates. But parents still expect an app and easy access.

Why Software Matters

Software Is Now Table Stakes

  • Parents expect it: No app = looks unprofessional = lose families to competitors
  • Directors need it: Manual billing, paper forms, and phone calls don't scale
  • Staff demand it: Younger teachers won't work somewhere with clipboard-based operations
  • Compliance requires it: Digital records for attendance, health, and incidents
  • Billing depends on it: AutoPay reduces late payments from 30% to under 5%

Why Now: Market Timing

Several Forces Are Creating a Window of Opportunity
1
Generational shift in directors. Baby Boomer directors who resisted technology are retiring. Millennial directors expect modern software and won't tolerate legacy systems.
2
Parent expectations have changed. Post-COVID, parents expect real-time updates, mobile apps, and digital payments. Centers without modern tech lose families.
3
Legacy platforms are showing their age. ProCare (1992), CampMinder (2001), and CampBrain (1994) have decades of technical debt. They can't easily rebuild for mobile-first.
4
Hybrid programs are growing. Preschools are adding summer camps for revenue. Camps want year-round engagement. Neither market's software supports both ‚Äî except Bloomily.
5
PE consolidation is underway. Roper bought ProCare. Bain invested in Lillio. DaySmart bought Sawyer. This signals market maturity and validates the opportunity ‚Äî but also means incumbents may get slower and more bureaucratic.
6
AI is creating new possibilities. Predictive staffing, arrival time forecasting, and automated communication are becoming possible. Legacy codebases can't easily add AI features; we can.

Risks & Challenges (Honest Assessment)

No opportunity is without risk. Here's an honest look at the challenges we face.

‚ö†Ô∏è Sales Cycle & Customer Acquisition
  • Directors are risk-averse. They won't switch software on a whim. References, case studies, and trust take time to build.
  • Switching costs are real. Migrating data, retraining staff, and updating parent accounts is painful. Inertia favors incumbents.
  • Decision cycles are slow. Budget decisions often happen annually. A "yes" in March might mean onboarding in August.
  • Word of mouth is everything. Directors talk to each other. One bad experience spreads fast.
üè¢ Competitive Response
  • Brightwheel has deep pockets. $600M+ raised means they can outspend us on marketing, support, and features.
  • Incumbents could add camps. Brightwheel or ProCare could bolt on camp features (though architectural limitations make this hard).
  • Camp players could add childcare. CampMinder could try to expand (same architectural challenge, but possible).
  • Price wars are possible. If we gain traction, competitors may drop prices to defend territory.
üì¶ Product & Execution
  • Feature parity gaps. We lack some features competitors have (payroll, cabin assignments, health center module). Prioritization is key.
  • Support scaling. Childcare directors expect responsive support. Scaling support while keeping quality is hard.
  • Reliability is non-negotiable. If check-in goes down during drop-off, trust is destroyed. Uptime must be exceptional.
  • Mobile app quality bar is high. Parents compare us to consumer apps. Anything clunky feels unacceptable.
üéØ Market Risks
  • Economic sensitivity. Childcare is semi-discretionary. Recessions lead to enrollment drops and center closures.
  • Regulatory changes. State licensing requirements and subsidy rules change. Software must adapt quickly.
  • Market concentration. PE firms are rolling up childcare centers into chains. Large chains may build or demand custom solutions.

Why We Think We Can Win Anyway

Our Advantages Outweigh the Risks

  • Architectural moat: Competitors can't easily serve both markets ‚Äî it's not a feature, it's a rebuild.
  • Greenfield segment: Hybrid organizations are underserved. We can win them without competing head-to-head.
  • Modern stack: We can ship features faster than legacy competitors drowning in technical debt.
  • Flat pricing: Directors love predictability. Per-child fees are a genuine pain point we solve.
  • Focus: Big competitors are spread thin. We can out-execute in our niche.
